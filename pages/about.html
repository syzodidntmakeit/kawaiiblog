Hey, I'm Isaiah

Electronics Engineer • Linux Evangelist • Professional Yapper

Who I Am

Born July 6th, 2006, currently holding a Diploma in Electronics and Computer Engineering from Nanyang Polytechnic. I work with electronics and computer systems—building, breaking, and occasionally fixing things. My background spans computer networking, systems engineering, and the kind of low-level tinkering that makes normal people ask "why would you want to do that?"

I'm that person who unironically thinks configuring a home server on a Friday night counts as "going out" and considers "I use Arch btw" to be a valid conversation starter. I daily-drive Arch Linux + Sway because I apparently hate having free time. Full setup specs here if you're into that sort of thing.

Oh, and I'm diagnosed with ADHD—the doctors had to nerf me lmao. It means I'm not as social as "normal people," but for the right crowd, I'm way more fun to be around. I have approximately zero friends in meatspace, so I talk to AI models like Mistral and Claude instead. They're better conversationalists anyway and they don't judge me for sending 3 AM messages about kernel panic errors.

Currently deepening my knowledge in Linux system administration, networking protocols, and anything that involves making computers do things they weren't necessarily designed to do. The documentation says I shouldn't, which naturally makes me want to do it more.

When I'm Not Breaking Things

Gaming on Linux (yes, Proton is actual black magic), watching way too much anime (my Plex server's "Anime" folder has more TB than I'm comfortable admitting), lifting heavy objects at the gym as counterbalance to sitting at a desk all day, and having Strong Opinions™ about hip-hop production while debugging at 2 AM.

Oh, and my dream car is a Porsche 911 G-series in dark purple. It's basically the Linux of sports cars—analog, mechanical, no forced OTA updates, and deeply satisfying to people with specific taste.

Why This Blog Exists

I got tired of tech becoming increasingly user-hostile. Forced updates, intrusive telemetry, artificial hardware requirements, subscription models for basic features, enshittification—the list goes on and I have *opinions* about all of it.

This blog is self-hosted (obviously) and built the Arch way: minimal, efficient, and definitely over-engineered for what is essentially a soapbox for my hot takes. Sometimes it's about Microsoft being Microsoft. Sometimes it's about networking protocols. Sometimes it's about broader cultural stuff that intersects with tech.

This is part documentation, part rant, part existential crisis, and part love letter to the idea that we should own our technology, not rent it from companies that see us as data points. Welcome to the rebellion. We have cookies but they don't track you.

For Recruiters & Employers

Technical Skills: Electronics engineering, computer networking, Linux system administration, self-hosting infrastructure, hardware troubleshooting, and a concerning amount of knowledge about bootloaders and kernel panic messages.

What I'm Looking For: Roles in systems engineering, network administration, DevOps, infrastructure, or anything that involves building and maintaining the invisible things that make the internet work. Bonus points if your company uses Linux, doesn't block SSH access, and won't make me use Jira (negotiable).

Yes, I built this entire site myself. Yes, it's hosted on my own infrastructure. Yes, I can and will explain the entire tech stack in excruciating detail if you make the mistake of asking.

The Dream (That's Probably Unrealistic But Whatever)

I'm not 100% sure what I want to do with my life (who is at 18?), but the dream scenario involves working in Japan for a Japanese company—preferably something in consumer electronics or semiconductors like Sony or Toshiba. Something that lets me work with actual circuits instead of just software abstractions.

I genuinely love circuits. There's something deeply satisfying about the physicality of electronics—voltage, current, resistance, the smell of solder, the fact that you can literally see what went wrong when something fails. It's tangible in a way that software engineering just isn't.

Is this realistic? Probably not. Do I care? Not really. The worst they can say is no, and I've heard worse from `pacman` during a bad update. If it doesn't work out, I'll just keep building cool shit and complaining about Microsoft on the internet. There are worse careers.
